In regard to the first motion on Johnson's part, happily the applicant, by counsel, consents to it and the court has the jurisdiction so to order. Happily, because ordinarily jurisdiction cannot be conferred or acquired merely by consent or convenience. The respondents are not both represented by counsel. The Minister appeared by counsel, but not the adjudicator, although duly served. Their interests are not diminished by the according of leave to intervene sought by Johnson.
[5]
Such a focus is particularly necessary where, as here, the federal tribunal's jurisdiction is attacked. If the tribunal will not defend its jurisdiction, Johnson must be permitted to intervene, and that would be so, even if the tribunal - the adjudicator - were to defend his jurisdiction. The adjudicator is a person whom the Minister entrusted to perform an inter partes adjudication, and it is difficult to understand why the Department of Justice seeks to avoid the provision of counsel, an expense which the adjudicator alone should not have to bear, in order to defend jurisdiction. Johnson alone is perhaps left to do that. Johnson seeks to intervene to defend her interests. Johnson surely has an interest in the outcome and could be seriously affected by it. She will probably present a different point of view from that of the adjudicator, and indeed, the only response if the adjudicator does not respond. She is permitted to be an intervenor herein, as if she were a party with all the rights and responsibilities of a party, including the right of appeal, and/or response to another party's appeal, if any. The style of cause shall be amended accordingly. This disposition deals with the first of Johnson's applications, but it does not dispose of the issue of compliance with rule 1602(3) which came into force on February 1, 1992.

The respondent's counsel asserts that the allegations made in the above-cited paragraphs have no place in this court. He states that in paragraphs 3, 8, 9 and 10 counsel for the applicant wants to relate to this court what evidence that deponent perceived to have been adduced before the adjudicator and his interpretation of it, or the interpretation he would like to put on the evidence and arguments presented to the adjudicator. If such a deposition would not be legitimate for a spectator at the adjudication, why should counsel be permitted to make such deposition?
[11]
Judicial review, says the intervenor's counsel, is confined to a record which has a narrowly defined base, generally: the initiating document, the award and the adjudicator's reasons if given with and as a part of the award. (At this point, consideration of the import of the
Canada Labour Code's
ss. 243(1) and (2) is deferred for a later passage in these reasons.) The applicant's principal and essential complaint is about the quantum of the award, the applicant having admitted that the intervenor was unjustly dismissed from her employment. Since that award's quantum is not shown to be so shocking or egregiously wrong, it is not judicially reviewable. After all, these proceedings are not, and cannot be, an appeal on the record, going classically to "law, evidence and the weight of evidence". Here, an error of law and an error of fact finding must be such as to exceed the tribunal's jurisdiction.
[12]
If the adjudicator committed an error of law, it must be shown that he applied such a wrong legal text as to have made an award which Parliament has never intended be made. That clearly is not the case here. Indeed, even in an appeal on the record, far short of which judicial review amounts in microscopic analysis, the appellate court or tribunal is bound not to disturb an award of damages which it, itself, would not have made - so long as such award be not grossly aberrant. This is not an appeal on the record, to repeat the obvious, for which Parliament made no provision, but rather a discretionary judicial review upon application to be supported by affidavits.
[13]
Paragraph 3 of the solicitor's impugned affidavit purports to summarize the intervenor's (i.e. complainant's) arguments to the adjudicator. Whether parts of paragraph 3 might be salvaged if recombined and differently expressed, matters not. It is simply irrelevant and improper on an application for judicial review. Is this unthinkable? The jurisprudence is mixed in result, some decisions leaving the sorting-out of impugned supporting affidavits to the judge who will adjudicate the application, such as
 <FRAGMENT_SUPPRESSED>  (an interlocutory matter in which much of the mixed jurisprudence is cited) (appeal, A-610-84, dismissed with costs January 10, 1985), and
 <FRAGMENT_SUPPRESSED>  (an originating application in which the affidavit was wholly struck out, citing supporting jurisprudence, with leave to file a new affidavit) (appeal, A-926-90, abandoned Nov. 13, 1991). Paragraph 3 will be struck.

The adjudicator adjudicated properly in regard to the evidence and arguments presented to him. Paragraphs 8 and 9 thresh old straw without demonstrating any error to be the subject of judicial review. In seeking to obtain a second hearing on the matter of reinstatement and in presenting apparently new evidence and in seeking to reargue by referring to the arguments put to the adjudicator the said paragraphs evince compositional, internal impropriety, but no error on the adjudicator's part. They too warrant being struck out, for they raise no jurisdictional error. The rest of lawyer Newman's paragraph 10 consists of his commentary on or reconstruction of what Johnson said in cross-examination and in answer to the adjudicator's questions about her conduct and knowledge concerning the recited passage of the exhibited policy manual. To what purpose is lawyer Newman's recitation of selected testimony by Johnson, even if his recitation be accurately reconstructed? The adjudicator dealt with those considerations unexceptionably at pp. 6 to 8 of his written adjudication decision. There is, objectively, nothing patently unreasonable about the adjudicator's treatment of the subject, and nothing patently unreasonable is alleged about the grounds in paragraph 3 of the applicant's notice of motion, which is confined to alleging error in law. Nor, indeed, did the applicant's counsel, the same Mr. Newman, allege, orally or in writing, that the adjudicator's consideration and conclusions on the termination-of-employment provisions of the manual were patently unreasonable.
[17]
It is true that paragraph 2 of the stated grounds in the applicant's notice of motion alleges that "the award of the adjudicator is patently unreasonable", but unless that allegation be patently demonstrable, and it is not, it is mere verbal filler and nothing more. If there be anything at all unreasonable about the adjudicator's award, which is not to suggest that there is, such unreasonableness is far, far from being "patent", meaning manifest or apparent to everybody as in the expression: "... hereby make these letters patent", patent being there an adjective. In the above considered expression, "patently" is the adverb.
[18]
All this is to say that paragraph 10 of Mr. Newman's affidavit does not demonstrate any crossing of the very high threshold test of "patently unreasonable", and in failing to do so it remains merely improper and vexatious. Paragraph 10 of Mr. Newman's affidavit shall be struck, and would deserve that fate even if it were not the applicant's counsel's deposition in support of the motion. Counsel argues that it (and paragraphs 3, 8 and 9) "are not intended in any way to deal with the merit of the decision the adjudicator made but rather to show that the adjudicator made an error of law and/or exceeded his jurisdiction in coming to the conclusions he did as disclosed by his decision". Paragraph 10, and the others, utterly fail to demonstrate any excess of jurisdiction, and, if they show an error of law, which is not to say they do show such, it must be in the circumstances, and is, very far from being such an egregious error as to render the proceedings or the award "patently unreasonable".

[20]
This is the point at which to consider the privative provision emplaced in s. 243 of the
Canada Labour Code
, R.S.C. 1985, c. L-2 (hereinafter, sometimes the
Code
). Remembering that the adjudicator in this case was appointed by the Minister pursuant to s. 242 of the
Code
, the succeeding provision runs as follows:
"243(1) Every order of an adjudicator appointed under subsection 242(1) is
final, and shall not be questioned or reviewed in any court
.
"243(2) No order shall be made, process entered or proceeding taken in any court, whether by way of injunction, certiorari, prohibition, quo warranto or otherwise, to question, review, prohibit or restrain an adjudicator in any proceedings of the adjudicator under s. 242." (Emphasis not in text)
It may be wondered why the court did not dismiss out of hand the applicant W.R.T.C.'s motion to quash the adjudicator's decision.
[21]
Parliament accords to this adjudicator and all adjudicators, the jurisdiction prescribed in the
Code's
s. 242(4), the W.R.T.C. having admitted unjust dismissal of Johnson. It runs:
"242(4) Where an adjudicator decides pursuant to subsection (3) that a person has been unjustly dismissed, the adjudicator may, by order, require the employer who dismissed the person to
(a) pay the person compensation not exceeding the amount of money that is equivalent to the remuneration that would, but for the dismissal, have been paid by the employer to the person;
(b) reinstate the person in his employ; and
(c) do any other like thing that it is equitable to require the employer to do in order to remedy or counteract any consequence of the dismissal."
If the adjudicator fairly and without proved bias, or proved apprehension of bias, operates within the four corners of s. 242, and especially s. 242(4), the adjudicator is complying with the will of Parliament and pursuant also to Parliament's will, s. 243 of the
Code
exacts that the adjudicator, despite any garden-variety or other errors of procedure, fact and law, short of patent unreasonableness, shall not be disturbed or reviewed.
[22]
The court presumes to transcend the protective provisions of s. 243 only if it be demonstrated that the adjudicator has so patently unreasonably stepped outside of the jurisdiction which Parliament conferred that the adjudicator can be clearly perceived to have perverted, evaded, avoided or contradicted the will of Parliament. After all, the High Court of Parliament can hardly be convened for such matters which are the ordinary grist for the adjudicatory mills of the superior courts. Parliament's protection of the adjudicators' process awards and decisions is simply intended to cover a multitude of adjudicators' "sins" so long as the result be not patently unreasonable.
[23]

Curial deference is more than just a fiction courts resort to when they are in agreement with the decisions of the tribunal. Mere disagreement with the result arrived at by the tribunal does not make that result 'patently unreasonable'. The courts must be careful to focus their inquiry on the existence of a rational basis for the decision of the tribunal, and not on their agreement with it. Here is the slightly variant, but "important respect" in which Sopinka, J., differs from his colleague, at pp. 1017-1018 S.C.C.:
"While I agree generally with La Forest, J., on the principles underlying the scope and standard of review of labour board decisions, I cannot agree that it is always necessary for the reviewing court to ignore its own view of the merits of the decision under review. Any adjudication upon the reasonableness of a decision must involve an evaluation of the merits. Reasonableness is not a quality that exists in isolation. When a court says that a decision under review is 'reasonable' or 'patently unreasonable' it is making a statement about the logical relationship between the grounds of the decision and premises thought by the court to be true. Without the reference point of an opinion (if not a conclusion) on the merits, such a relative statement cannot be made.
"I share La Forest, J.'s, opinion of the importance of curial deference in the review of specialist tribunals' decisions. But, in my view, curial deference does not enter the picture until the court finds itself in disagreement with the tribunal. Only then is it necessary to consider whether the error (so found) is within or outside the boundaries of reasonableness. The test is, as La Forest, J., points out, citing
 <FRAGMENT_SUPPRESSED> , a 'severe test'. But even here an appreciation of the merits is not irrelevant. Lamer, J., speaking for himself and McIntyre, J., in <FRAGMENT_SUPPRESSED> , stated at pp. 494-495:
'... though all errors do not lead to unreasonable findings, every unreasonable finding results from an error (whether of law, fact, or a combination of the two), which is unreasonable.'

[[1988] 2 S.C.R. 1048] (at pp. 1088-1089):
'
At this stage, the court examines not only the wording of the enactment conferring jurisdiction on the administrative tribunal, but the purpose of the statute creating the tribunal, the reason for its existence, the area of expertise of its members and the nature of the problem before the tribunal.
At this initial stage a pragmatic or functional analysis is just as suited to a case in which an error is alleged in the interpretation of a provision limiting the administrative tribunal's jurisdiction: in a case where a patently unreasonable error is alleged on a question within the jurisdiction of the tribunal, as in a case where simple error is alleged regarding a provision limiting that jurisdiction, the first step involves determining the tribunal's jurisdiction.' [Emphasis added]
. . . . .
"Adopting a pragmatic and functional approach to the construction of these provisions, I have come to the conclusion that Parliament did not intend to confer jurisdiction on the Board with respect to the labour relations of employees who are not members of the Public Service. The Board by an error of law has assumed jurisdiction that it was not intended to have. Its decision is, therefore, reviewable."
Mr. Justice Sopinka, for the majority explained quite clearly why they did not defer to the decision of the P.S.S.R.B. in that
 <FRAGMENT_SUPPRESSED>  definition of the word 'employee' provided by s. 2, is practically decisive in this case. Section 33 is intended to enable the Board to resolve any question as to whether an employee or class of employees is or is not included in a bargaining unit. In the absence of a definition of 'employee', it could be argued that the Board could determine who is an employee on the basis of tests that are generally employed in labour matters. These tests are customarily employed to resolve a dispute as to whether a person is an employee or an independent contractor. The express definition of 'employee', however, shows a clear intention by Parliament that it has decided the category of employee over which the Board is to have jurisdiction. It is restricted to persons employed in the Public Service and who are not covered by the
Canada Labour Code
. The Board's function by the very words of s. 33 is not to determine who is an employee but rather whether employees who come within the definition provided, are included in a particular bargaining unit."
However one looks at the P.S.S.R.B.'s conduct in this <FRAGMENT_SUPPRESSED> case, it is patently obvious that the P.S.S.R.B. blundered outside of the jurisdiction which Parliament intended by enactment to confer on that board.
[24]
Keeping in mind the adjudicator's statutory jurisdiction conferred by s. 242(4) of the
Code
, that is, to order the employer who unjustly dismissed a person, to (a) pay the person compensation not exceeding the amount of money that is equivalent to the remuneration that would, but for the dismissal, have been paid by the employer to the person; and ... (c) do any other like thing that it is equitable to require the employer to do in order to remedy or counteract
any
consequence of the dismissal, the court here finds no reason to go behind the privative provisions of ss. 243(1) and (2) of the
Code
. Indeed the court may even stretch its compass here into that "extra-statutory" reference to reasonableness by stating that the adjudicator's order is neither inherently, nor shown to be, "patently unreasonable" in the circumstances. Therefore, even if one were to accept the solicitor's affidavit, one simply could not, to this point of consideration, circumvent the privative clauses expressed in ss. 243(1) and (2) of the
Code
.
[25]
There is yet another ground upon which the W.R.T.C. seeks to have the adjudicator's order quashed; and this ground could be so serious, if established, as to bring down the adjudicator's order. The ground is that of bias, manifested as racial prejudice, alleged on the part of the adjudicator. If proved as actual bias at the time of the adjudication, or if objective, reasonable apprehension of bias be proved, such would ipso facto atomize the adjudicator's jurisdiction. No one could acquiesce in the proposition that Parliament would have intended that such a defective adjudicator would exercise adjudicatory jurisdiction over and between the parties, much less that his or her jurisdiction should be cosseted by a statutory privative clause.
[26]
As noted by Mr. Justice Sopinka in
 <FRAGMENT_SUPPRESSED>  decision under review. In the case at bar, the applicant's counsel indicated at the hearing of this matter that the whole of his evidence for the W.R.T.C. is expressed in his impugned affidavit. On that basis, this court can properly, according to Sopinka, J., and Lamer, now C.J.C., and easily form its own view of the merits of the decision under review in the light of the allegations of the adjudicator's possible "predisposition against natives which may have caused him prefer the complainant [Johnson] over the applicant [W.R.T.C.]", as alleged in paragraph 7 of the impugned affidavit sworn by the W.R.T.C.'s counsel.

Attached to counsel's said affidavit is Exhibit "H", a copy of a letter which he wrote and addressed to the adjudicator on December 16, 1991. The passage which could relate, distantly, to an allegation of some "predisposition against" the W.R.T.C., even although no such allegation is specifically levied against the adjudicator therein, is on page 3 and it runs thus:
"I wish to make one other comment in respect to two comments which you made during the course of the hearing. The comments which you made are as follows:

Yes, it is true that "concerns related to racial origin" and termination of employment "for racial reasons" are specified in that passage. That passage however boldly tells the adjudicator that he has apparently misconstrued the evidence adduced before him, or that he seems to have formed a view not supportable by such evidence. The writer, Mr. Newman, does not levy any accusation or reproach of bias in the nature of racial prejudice. No such bias can be inferred in these circumstances according to the usual judicial standards for finding such bias.

The only evidence of such bias as is alleged, is that expressed in the lawyer's affidavit which runs as follows:

Counsel for the W.R.T.C., the affiant of the above quoted affidavit passage, recognized that paragraph 6 constitutes the grossest hearsay, which is, in fact a breach of rule 332(1); and of like rules of the other superior courts in Canada. He indicated that his associate, Louise Lamb, could and would swear to her own affidavit. That affidavit would be subject to the same disabilities as is counsel's own affidavit, since Ms. Lamb was also the W.R.T.C.'s counsel before the adjudicator, as Mr. Newman averred, and she is his associate in their law firm. Even if Ms. Lamb's allegation could be properly proved to be true, does it evince prejudice or sympathy with the much publicized plight of some aboriginal persons in Manitoba?
[31]
Do the allegations in the lawyer's affidavit, if true, show bias or a reasonable apprehension of bias? No. The first allegation is found in paragraph 4(a). In light of other evidence before the adjudicator, the alleged speculation would likely occur to any intelligent, shrewd and sensitive person. Given the current climate of lofty support for the "first nations" campaign for the racial segregation of peoples inherent in aboriginal self-government, the speculation would come as easily as it might in other instances of an employer being a Celtic Literacy Society, Sons of Erin, Club La Verendrye, Ukrainian National Home or any other employer distinguished by racial or ethnic purposes. Given the W.R.T.U.'s admission of wrongful dismissal, the adjudicator may have simply been enquiring if there would be other evidence which would bear on the award. Even if such were not the adjudicator's intent, and remembering that this affidavit, even if true, is not properly before the court, the allegation in paragraph 4(a) does not taint the adjudicator's jurisdiction. Many persons in Canada who are not of the aboriginal peoples are nevertheless natives because they were born in Canada. That the adjudicator knew that the West Region Tribal Council was established by and for aboriginal people of whom Johnson is apparently not one, would not even require great perspicacity on his part. That goes utterly no distance toward establishing any prejudice either against them or against her. That complaint is a nonentity.
[32]
Paragraph 4(b) alleged facts which may be related to those of the preceding complaint. No one here has asserted that aboriginal organizations are legally or morally bound to hire Euro-Canadians or bound not to give preference in hiring to Aborigino-Canadians. As with the Celtic, Francophone or Slavic organizations earlier above mentioned there could be required for employment an appropriate justifiable language skill. If that were so, an injudicious observation, but not a mark of prejudice, on the adjudicator's part would be going from one such position (if it were so) where Johnson was wrongfully or unjustly dismissed, to another such position, or even seeking such a position would be like "going from the frying pan into the fire". Perhaps the adjudicator ought never to have said that, if he did, but it does not condemn him of prejudice, nor strip him of jurisdiction.

A good standard by which any such matter of alleged bias or prejudice against a party to proceedings may be judged is that formulated by Mr. Justice de Grandpr√© in
Committee for Justice and  <FRAGMENT_SUPPRESSED> , at pp. 394-395 S.C.R. For himself, Martland and Judson, JJ., de Grandpr√©, J., adopted and ratified the norm expressed by the Federal Court of Appeal, which, shorn of all else mentioned by the learned judge boils down to this essence: "what would an
informed

By that standard, it is easy to hold that there is now shown to be even any reasonable apprehension of bias on the adjudicator's part. Of course, it would be quite otherwise if the adjudicator were shown to be a supporter of those who denigrate and mock non-Euro-Canadians as noted recently in this court in

Here no prima facie case is demonstrated of bias or even a reasonable apprehension thereof. No denial of natural justice has been shown. The applicant's effort in this regard is simply futile.
[36]
There is yet another ground upon which the applicant's attempt to strip the adjudicator of jurisdiction is also rendered futile. There is a nonspecific, but common-sense, limitation upon allegations of bias or prejudice because they are easy to levy after the event, but not so easy to prove - and the taint of such allegations may be impossible to avoid, even if the prejudice be not proved. Therefore, in jurisprudence the law requires prompt complaint. The counsel for the W.R.T.C. cited
Re Rosenfeld and College of Physicians & Surgeons
(1970), 11 D.L.R.(3d) 148 (Ont. H.C.), where a differently constituted committee recommended a penalty. It had no jurisdiction, so, the order imposing that penalty was a nullity, despite counsel's "not suggesting that they were not qualified to sit". That jurisprudence is distinguishable from the case at bar. A tribunal without jurisdiction ab initio will inevitably produce futility and nullity ab initio, but where it is alleged that the tribunal, as constituted, wielded undoubted jurisdiction, but then lost it through misconduct such as evincing prejudice, the complaint of prejudice is to be made promptly in order to be credible.
[37]
A nullity ab initio can be perceived and declared almost at any later time for it never was of any effect, and demonstrably so. However when one alleges a denial of natural justice by means of alleged prejudice, one should - nay, must - so allege promptly, for effluxion of time can render such allegation not objectively demonstrable at all. The allegation of prejudice should not be secretly harboured, but made public immediately, thereby hoping to catch the tribunal "red handed", so to speak, in its prejudice and misconduct. So it is that the posture of waiting to discover whether one wins the contention before the adjudicator, prepared to make no allegation of prejudice if one does win, and complaining of alleged prejudice as a means of trying to avoid a confirmed loss, is abusive and to be discouraged.
[38]
It is stated in Kavanagh
A Guide to Judicial Review
(2nd [and last] Ed. 1984], Carswell, c. 3 "Bias and Delegation of Function", at p. 49:
"(j)
Waiver of Objection to Bias
"By failing to raise an objection concerning bias during the tribunal proceedings, the right to complain of it may be waived on judicial review. [
 <FRAGMENT_SUPPRESSED>  (Sask.)] Bias only makes proceedings voidable, not void. It is not entirely clear under what circumstances a statutory disqualification, not merely declarative of a common law disqualification, may be waived. [de Smith,
Judicial Review of Administrative Action
(4th Ed.), pp. 275-276]"
[39]
In the work by Jones and de Villars,
Principles of Administrative Law
, 1985, Carswell, c. 13, "Prerogative Remedies", in compartment 7(a), contains a short study of acquiescence, on p. 373. It runs, in part:
"Acquiescence or implied waiver may occur when no objection is made to the tribunal's jurisdiction although the defect was apparent. Where there is a breach of the rules of natural justice or procedural fairness, continued participation in the proceedings may deprive the applicant of his right to a prerogative remedy. For example, the court may refuse relief where the applicant raises an allegation of bias after the proceedings have gone against him, but knew of all the facts relating to bias before or at the time of the hearing."
The authors thereupon cite a considerable volume of jurisprudence in support of the above recited passage.

Following Mr. Justice Sopinka's notion cited earlier above, that the court really cannot - and should not - avoid forming some view of the merits, and concluding that not even a scintilla of reasonable apprehension of bias resides in the worst the W.R.T.C.'s lawyers can offer, the court will not accept their complaint as formulated no matter who swears to it and no matter who acts as the W.R.T.C.'s counsel in this matter. The complaints against the investigator are trivial and the intervenor Johnson ought not to be put to the trouble and the expense of responding further to them in order to maintain her judgment.

This matter stands in this posture. There is no basis whatever for holding that the adjudicator's decision is patently unreasonable. There is no good reason shown here to subvert or circumvent the privative provision which Parliament enacted in s. 243 of the
Canada Labour Code
. No bias, prejudice or denial of natural justice is, or could be shown on the proposed evidence brought before the court in the impugned affidavit of the W.R.T.C.'s counsel. The proposed evidence is trivial, if not abusive. Finally the affidavit of the applicant's counsel, Mark Newman, sworn February 26, 1992 and filed February 28, 1992, must be struck out; and in substantive particular the court finds that its paragraphs 3 through 10 are improper in the circumstances and they must be struck out irrespective of other considerations. The affidavit shall be struck out.
[42]
Because the W.R.T.C.'s application for an order "quashing, setting aside and declaring invalid the decision of Adjudicator Booth" is supported by no other affidavit than the impugned and now-struck affidavit of Mark Newman, it cannot stand. The court listened carefully to both principal counsels, carefully perused their subsequent written submissions and the doctrine and jurisprudence cited, and the court has deliberated several months now on this case. In view of the considerations expressed in these reasons, there appears to be no good reason to extend the time to permit the W.R.T.C.'s counsel to obtain new affidavits. Without a finding of patent unreasonableness in the adjudicator's award, and no jurisdiction-dissolving bias, prejudice or other denial of natural justice, and in view of Parliament's clear and persistent intent expressed in s. 243 of the
Canada Labour Code
, the court must really dismiss the W.R.T.C.'s attack upon the adjudicator's decision. The circumstances expressed and considered herein provide an ample basis for such an exercise of discretion.
